01 · Literature Survey

What the field has explored so far.

Recent research has demonstrated machine learning's potential across many corners of Ayurveda — from constitutional classification and disease prediction to herb recommendation and conversational Q&A. Yet most efforts remain siloed, monolingual, and disconnected from authoritative classical sources.

01 · PRAKRITI

Constituent balancing with ML

Madaan and Goyal (2020) used Random Forest, Naive Bayes, and Decision Tree on Prakriti questionnaires, achieving 93.05% accuracy in predicting Vata–Pitta–Kapha imbalance.

IEEE Access · 2020
02 · PRAKRITI

Phenotype-based Prakriti recap

Tiwari et al. (2017) showed that Ayurveda constitution types can be recapitulated through ML on phenotypic traits, validating constitutional categories computationally.

PLOS ONE · 2017
03 · DEEP LEARNING

Deep learning for Prakriti

Khatua et al. (2023) classified Ayurveda constitution types using deep learning, achieving improved accuracy over traditional ML approaches.

Soft Computing · 2023
04 · DISEASE

Parkinson's via dosha analysis

Majhi et al. (2023) developed an ML-based Parkinson's prediction system through Ayurvedic dosha analysis — strong precedent for disease-specific dosha modelling.

Int. J. Ayurvedic Medicine · 2023
05 · MEDICINE

Decision Tree-based herb recommendation

Vijay M et al. (2024) presented an Ayurvedic Medicine Recommendation System using Decision Tree algorithms with symptom-based analysis, reaching 95.34% test accuracy.

ICERCS · 2024
06 · MEDICINE

Ontology-based personalised herbs

Promkot et al. (2019) proposed a personalised traditional medicine recommender using ontology modelling and rule inference — adaptable to Ayurveda.

IEEE ICCCS · 2019
07 · MEDICINE

AyurVedaMitra

Varsha et al. (2025) built a mobile Ayurvedic platform using CNN and Random Forest, demonstrating ensemble learning for precision medicine in traditional contexts.

IEEE IITCEE · 2025
08 · DIET

Ayurveda-based recipe recommendation

Ismayilova et al. (2024) mined 350,000+ recipes and used NLP + KNN to recommend dishes by dosha compatibility — a precedent for Ayurvedic dietary ML.

Preprint · 2024
09 · DIET

Ayurvedic concepts of food

Sharma et al. (2007) and Singh (2007) detail rasa, virya, vipaka, and pathya–apathya principles — foundational sources for any computational diet system.

J. Altern. Complement. Med.
10 · QA

AyurMate & AyUR-bot

Recent rule-based and RAG-style Ayurvedic chatbots (2024–2025) achieve fluent QA but lack source-grounded citations and multilingual support.

IEEE AIDE · 2025
11 · QA

Sinhala Ayurvedic chatbot

Jayalath et al. (2019) integrated CNN-based plant recognition with a Sinhala AIML chatbot — innovative but rule-driven, with no semantic retrieval.

IEEE ICAC · 2019
12 · LOCAL

Sri Lankan Ayurvedic skin diseases

Silva et al. (2024) identified human skin diseases with ML using Sri Lankan Ayurveda — a closely related local effort applying ML to traditional medicine.

IEEE ICIT · 2024
02 · Research Gap

Where existing work falls short.

⚬

Constitution and disease are studied separately

Most research focuses either on Prakriti classification or on isolated disease prediction — never both, integrated into a single decision pipeline.

⚬

Classical texts remain unstructured

Charaka Samhita, Madhava Nidana, and Ashtanga Hridaya hold rich symptom–disease–herb relationships, but no machine-readable structured corpus exists for Sri Lankan Ayurveda.

⚬

No multi-script OCR for Sanskrit + Sinhala

Standard OCR tools cannot accurately read ligature-heavy Sanskrit Devanagari or classical Sinhala fonts — blocking automated knowledge extraction at scale.

⚬

No cross-lingual retrieval for Sinhala/Singlish

Existing Ayurvedic chatbots support English alone. Sri Lankan students and practitioners often use Sinhala or code-mixed Singlish, leaving them unserved.

⚬

Diet systems oversimplify Ayurvedic principles

Most digital diet tools reduce dosha–food relationships to binary favour/avoid lists, ignoring rasa, virya, vipaka, guna, and contextual factors like agni, mood, and season.

⚬

Single-output systems

Existing tools predict or recommend — none combine prediction, medicine, diet, and source-grounded Q&A into one constitutionally-aware platform.

⚬

Lack of practitioner validation

Many computational Ayurveda projects skip practitioner validation, weakening clinical reliability and trust in their recommendations.

⚬

Hallucination in generative QA

Generative chatbots produce fluent answers without source citations, leading to fabricated content with no way to verify against authoritative texts.

⚬

Limited Sri Lankan Ayurvedic AI

Despite Ayurveda's cultural prominence in Sri Lanka, very few digital, intelligent systems exist that respect local Hela Wedakama principles and serve local communities.

03 · Research Problem

The problem we're solving.

How can we build an integrated AI system that respects classical Ayurvedic principles — particularly Prakriti and dosha imbalance — while delivering accurate, personalised disease prediction, herbal recommendation, dietary planning, and source-grounded advisory support to Sri Lankans who lack easy access to qualified practitioners?

Cultural fidelity Preserve authentic Ayurvedic principles in every recommendation, validated by practitioners.
Computational rigour Apply rigorous ML, NLP, and document intelligence with proper evaluation.
Real accessibility Serve users in Sinhala, English, and Singlish — including low-connectivity regions.

05 · Methodology

From classical text to predictive model.

Our approach follows a structured pipeline shared across all four modules — gathering authentic data, processing it with NLP and document intelligence, engineering features, training and validating models, and integrating the results into a usable interface.

  1. 01

    Data collection

    Digitise classical Ayurvedic texts (Charaka Samhita, Madhava Nidana, Ashtanga Hridaya), collect Prakriti questionnaire responses, gather anonymised consultation records from Nawinna and Rajagiriya hospitals, and mine validated Ayurvedic dietary manuals.

  2. 02

    Document intelligence & NLP

    Apply multi-script OCR (Sanskrit Devanagari + Sinhala) and Named Entity Recognition tuned for medical terminology to extract symptom–disease, disease–herb, and food–dosha relationships from classical sources.

  3. 03

    Feature engineering

    Encode symptoms, classify foods by rasa/virya/vipaka/guna, weight features by constitutional context, score severity (mild / moderate / severe), and integrate dosha imbalance as predictive signal.

  4. 04

    Model training

    Train and compare Random Forest, SVM, Decision Tree, KNN, and Neural Network classifiers across modules. Build a Hybrid RAG (FAISS + BM25) retriever for the Q&A module with cross-lingual embeddings.

  5. 05

    Expert validation

    Validate predictions, herbal mappings, and dietary recommendations through consultation with qualified Ayurvedic practitioners and dieticians. Compare model performance with and without constitutional features.

  6. 06

    Integration & deployment

    Build a unified web interface where the four modules — disease prediction, medicine, diet, Q&A — share user context, integrate via REST APIs, and deliver personalised outputs with confidence scores and citations.
